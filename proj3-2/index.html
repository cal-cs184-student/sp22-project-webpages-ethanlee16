Assignment 3-2: Pathtracing

By Annie Wang and Ethan Lee

In this assignment, we explored more advanced material reflection and refraction effects by building special-case BSDFs for mirror and glass surfaces. We also studied the effects of using a thin-lens camera on the depth-of-field effect in our rendering. We encountered challenges with implementing the refraction parts, especially with keeping track of the direction and angles of incident and outgoing rays, but we gained a more intuitive sense of reflection and refraction geometrically by drawing out our rays visually.

Beginning at a ray depth of 1, we see the colors of all surfaces (including walls and the surface of the spheres) from one-bounce radiance (direct illumination).

At a ray depth of 2, we see the effect of reflections, as the ceiling in the same plane of the light source receives some color, and the highly reflective sphere on the left produces a mirror effect. This occurs at a depth of 2 because it requires at least 2 bounces of light to hit a reflected surface and off the sphere to meet the camera.

At a ray depth of 3, we see the effects of refraction more clearly in the sphere on the right, as a warped view of the sphere’s surroundings appears in the sphere. We also see the shadows lighten and become more detailed as light passes through the spheres and bounces back. This occurs at a depth of 3 because it requires at least 3 bounces of light to hit the sphere, move through the sphere, and bounce back towards the camera.

At a ray depth of 4, we see the reflection of the sphere on the right appear more accurately from the sphere on the left; instead of appearing completely dark it shows the detail of the sphere’s reflection). A bright spot on the right wall also appears as a part of the light in the scene passing through the right sphere concentrating on a certain point on the wall.

At a ray depth of 5 and 100, no major visual changes occur, but the reflective and refractive effects become more detailed and appear with more radiance in general.

Part 4: Depth of Field

In an ideal pinhole camera model, the radius of the lens that we are tracing rays from is 0 (all rays converge to one point). This results in all parts of the image being in focus. In a thin-lens camera model, on the other hand, the ray can hit any part of the surface of the lens, which is a surface area that we sample from to create the image. This results in only some parts of the image being in focus depending on the plane of focus.
