Project 4: Cloth Simulation

By Annie Wang and Ethan Lee

Introduction

In this project, we built a mass-spring system to model a falling piece of cloth, adding features like collisions with objects and collisions with other points of the cloth to make it more life-like. We learned about how closely you can approximate the behavior of real-life objects by keeping track of the involved physics and encountered some difficulties in doing so, especially in understanding the vector directions of various point mass position corrections.

Part 2

Very low ks: resulted in folds appearing from the pinned corners towards the center of the cloth. The cloth also elongated more.

Very high ks: resulted in a more rigid appearance of the cloth, the cloth fell almost uniformly from start to finish.

Very low density: similar to a high ks, resulted in a more rigid appearance while falling.

Very high density: similar to a low ks, folds from pinned points to center appeared. Cloth seemed to unwrap more readily towards its resting state.

Changing the damping appeared to change the rate at which the cloth fell (lower damping meant that the cloth unwrapped faster due to external forces). Changing the damping factor to 0 resulted in some elastic behavior, where the cloth would blow back past the resting state and start swinging back and forth.

The low-density cloth seemed to fold on itself with properties like a large sheet of paper. It maintained a smooth shape while falling and created large folds as it fell. By contrast, the high-density cloth folded on itself with many creases, almost like a very dense fluid. The resulting rest position had significantly more folds than the low-density cloth.

And an example with a high ks value:

Similarly to the high-density cloth, the low-ks cloth resulted in more complex folds while it fell. The folding that was occurring underneath the cloth as it was collapsing on itself did not affect the structure of the top half of the cloth very much (until it reached the bottom).

The high-ks cloth by contrast resulted in larger, more wavy folds occurring in the cloth as it fell, similar to the low-density cloth. However, the small variations in the cloth resulted in a less uniform fall than the paper-like behavior of low-density cloth. The high-ks cloth had properties more similar to cloth than paper.

A shader program is a kind of program that executes during the graphics pipeline and is capable of processing individual vertexes and fragments with different operations.

An example of a vertex shader might be one that varies the surface of an object with a rough material, moving positions and normal vectors in ways that make the object appear less smooth. A fragment shader can be used to change the rasterized appearance of fragments of the sampled screen space, for example, to sample the color values of a certain object from a texture image.

Blinn-Phong shading is a kind of shading model that combines ambient, specular, and diffuse lighting effects – in particular, Blinn-Phong is distinguished by varying the specular light intensity with respect to the viewing direction rather than the reflection direction. Bump mapping is a type of fragment shader that modifies the normal vectors used for lighting effects using a texture that acts as a height map for calculating the new normal vectors. Displacement mapping also applies this fragment shading effect, but also adds a vertex shader that moves the vertexes by the same value in the height map (scaled by some factor).

The difference can be seen in the rendering results above; displacement mapping has the same lighting effects but starts taking on a slightly different shape in the sphere and cloth, forming ridges in the geometry where the height is lower in the texture.
